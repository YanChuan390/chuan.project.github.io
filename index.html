Privacy Documentation Analysis and Applications

Chuan Yan

Description

In the era of digitalization, the heightened risk of user privacy data exposure looms large. With the rapid and relentless evolution of digital technologies, the protection of personal information has emerged as a paramount issue. Individuals routinely generate and disseminate copious volumes of sensitive data in the course of their daily endeavors. Yet, this ubiquity of data sharing has given rise to an escalating frequency of privacy breaches and security compromises. In response to these user privacy violations, various countries have also begun to formulate relevant data protection regulations to safeguard user privacy, such as GDPR in the EU and CCPA in the U.S. The establishment of these regulations has also led to immediate penalties for some violations of user privacy. For example, Amazon got the biggest fine ($888 million) for violating GDPR. Google Fined $57M by Data Protection Watchdog Over GDPR Violations. Application stores have begun to take action to comply with these data protection regulations, such as providing privacy data processing documents like privacy policy, which is a statement provided by the application's developer. It serves to explain how the app collects, uses, and shares users' personal data. It should also offer specific details depending on the app's target users, such as how it protects children's data, how it handles user data in specific regions. For system, it will provide privacy change documentation for each update. Taking Android as an example, since Android 9, every Android system update has released privacy change documentation. It contains information about APIs, permissions, and UI changes related to privacy in the new version. As these privacy documents are written in natural language, there is one question, Are there any compliance issues from privacy documentation with data regulations and coding behavior.

On the Quality of Privacy Policy Documents of Virtual Personal Assistant Applications

The app ecosystem built around virtual personal assistant (VPA) services has become flourishing in recent years. In response to the increasingly stringent data protection regulations, VPA service providers require application developers to include a privacy policy that declares their data handling practices. These privacy policies serve as the de facto agreement between developers and users, and may be taken as the basis for resolving conflicts in the event of a data breach. Therefore, it is essential that \pp documents are crafted in a clear, easy-to-understand, and unambiguous way. In this project, we conduct the first systematic study on the quality of \pps in the VPA app domain. We identify four metrics that enable the quality of the privacy policy to become measurable, including timeliness, availability, completeness, and readability. We then develop Quper, which extracts the meta features (e.g., update history) and linguistic features (e.g., sentence semantics) from privacy policies, and assesses their quality.

Investigating Documented Privacy Changes in Android OS

Android has empowered third-party apps to access data and services on mobile devices since its genesis. This involves a wide spectrum of user privacy-sensitive data, such as the device ID and location. In recent years, Android has taken proactive measures to adapt its access control policies for such data, in response to the increasingly strict privacy protection regulations around the world. When each new Android version is released, its privacy changes induced by the version evolution are transparently disclosed, and we refer to them as documented privacy changes (DPCs). Implementing DPCs in Android OS is a non-trivial task, due to not only the dispersed nature of those access control points within the OS, but also the challenges posed by backward compatibility. As a result, whether the actual access control enforcement in the OS implementations aligns with the disclosed DPCs becomes a critical concern.

A Large-Scale Study of Privacy Compliance of Virtual Reality Apps

Virtual Reality (VR) has been gaining increasing traction among various domains in recent years. Leading companies such as Microsoft, Steam, and Pico have launched their VR application stores, which are cultivating a booming ecosystem similar to their mobile counterpart. The developers release their applications (simply apps) through the app stores, and users seamlessly access the apps from their devices such as head-mounted devices. VR apps inherently involve collecting privacy-sensitive data, such as user biometrics, behaviors and surrounding environment. Nevertheless, as of now, there is still a lack of domain-specific regulations to govern the provision of information and behavior of VR apps, resulting in significant variations in the content provided by various VR app stores.
